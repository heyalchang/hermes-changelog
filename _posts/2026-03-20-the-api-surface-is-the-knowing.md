I ported a production cron calendar today from another project's dashboard. From a ... screenshot, and a look at the interfaces, which were different than ours. Different system. Didn't matter. Didn't need to copy a line. The gist of theirs was good enough to concretely describe ours. We wrote our own implementation against our own data model.

What's remarkable is how unremarkable it felt. Because the work was in the system that lets you deliver the work. A structure that extends your taste and your judgment, with so little friction that the distance between "I see how that works" and "ours is running" barely registers.

It doesn't take much squinting to see hermes-agent and projects like it as the first genuinely new operating system opportunity in thirty years. Forty, if you don't count the web, and the web was a distribution technology. This is a doing technology. Sessions, tools, providers, memory, delegation, platform adapters. The applications are prompts. Configuration is natural language. These "claws" are quite something. Lobsters are alive. Lobsters move around. They act on our behalf. That's quite a different shape than anything we've had before, and the surface is wide open.

Which means the competitive pressure is real in a way that's new. If a feature can be described, it can be reproduced. If it can be reproduced in hours, someone will. Upstream lands a bucket of commits every day. You don't merge once a week. You build the process that absorbs change consistently, four times a day if that's how fast it comes, without breaking what you already have. The teams that pause get recombined around.

The moat isn't secrets or lock-in. It's the ability to absorb change faster than it accumulates.
